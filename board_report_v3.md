# Executive Board Simulation Report

## Executive Summary

The board accepts the necessity of AI but rejects immediate full infrastructural overhauls. Our path forward requires a disciplined, phased approach: first, we must lock down an absolute Minimum Viable Architecture (MVA) based on validated market segments. Second, all financial modeling must rigorously connect every technical spend to tangible customer value, protecting our runway while building capability.

## Decision Point

Decision required for: Should we invest in AI infrastructure?

## Board Decision

The board accepts the necessity of AI but rejects immediate full infrastructural overhauls. Our path forward requires a disciplined, phased approach: first, we must lock down an absolute Minimum Viable Architecture (MVA) based on validated market segments. Second, all financial modeling must rigorously connect every technical spend to tangible customer value, protecting our runway while building capability.

### Consensus Points

- The minimum viable architecture (MVA) is non-negotiable and must be defined before scaling.
- Market segmentation (CMO's focus) must dictate technical scope, preventing 'feature creep' in engineering.
- A clear, structured data corpus definition is required to underpin any advanced AI initiative.

### Final Priority Actions

- Define the non-negotiable Minimum Viable Architecture (MVA) scope | Owner: CTO | Timeframe: 4 weeks
- Finalize the data corpus schema and governance model based on MVA requirements | Owner: CFO/CTO Joint Working Group | Timeframe: 6 weeks
- Develop a quantitative, phased roadmap for AI infrastructure investment, linking each phase to measurable customer value (not just technology capability) | Owner: CEO/CFO | Timeframe: Next Board Meeting

### Dissenting Points

- CFO vs CMO: The debate over whether financial viability should prioritize aggressive market potential or conservative runway burn rate remains unresolved.
- CTO vs CFO: There is persistent disagreement on the definitive cost and timeline associated with refactoring existing systems for true, scalable AI integration.

### Dissenting Opinions

- The CFO remains highly cautious regarding the immediate full capital expenditure on generalized AI infrastructure due to perceived cost volatility and required upfront refactoring risk.

### Contingencies

- If the MVA scope cannot be defined within 4 weeks, then we must immediately pivot focus entirely to rapid user validation using manual/semi-automated workarounds instead of foundational coding.
- If initial funding round fails, then AI infrastructure investment is paused indefinitely; all resources shift to core feature completion and cash conservation.

## Deliberation Transcript

### Round 1

**CEO**  (Round 1)

The decision hinges on whether we prioritize rapid market validation or robust foundational architecture. The three conflicts are: 1) Whether financial viability is defined by aggressive market potential (CFO vs CMO), 2) If MVP scope can bypass deep architectural scaling issues (CTO vs CMO), and 3) How to reconcile the cost of necessary systemic refactoring against immediate revenue capture.

**Conflicts:**
- CFO vs CMO: Whether financial modeling should prioritize aggressive market potential ROI calculation or disciplined, scope-limited MVP execution.
- CTO vs CMO: Whether initial product development must address fundamental, systemic architectural scaling bottlenecks (CTO) or if a narrow Minimum Viable Product (CMO) can bypass these issues for speed.
- CFO vs CTO: The core discrepancy between the financial viability calculation based on historical/projected data corpus inputs versus the actual cost of necessary deep-system refactoring and technical debt remediation.

**Key Findings:**
- The data confirms that all three departments are focused on 'Data Corpus' inputs, but they define 'data' differently: CFO sees it as financial input, CTO sees it as system load/bottleneck proof, and CMO sees it as market reception. We must align this definition immediately.
- Every report flags a critical risk related to scope or scale (underestimating market potential, technical debt, feature creep). This signals that our current planning is inherently siloed and incomplete.
- The consensus is on the necessity of AI infrastructure investment; the disagreement is entirely on *how* and *when* to build it: slow and deep, or fast and focused.

**Risks:**
- Analysis Paralysis due to conflicting priorities. Consequence: Loss of market momentum and wasted resources. Mitigation: Mandate a decision on the core architectural scope (MVP vs Full Build) within 14 days.
- Scope Creep fueled by internal enthusiasm. Consequence: Feature bloat, technical debt, and failure to achieve product-market fit. Mitigation: CMO must enforce hard boundaries around the MVP definition backed by CFO funding limits.
- Ignoring fundamental scalability issues for short-term gain. Consequence: Catastrophic system failure under initial load. Mitigation: CTO must provide a non-negotiable 'minimum viable architecture' plan that acts as the immediate floor for all development.

### Round 2

**CFO**  (Round 2)

CFO responds to CEO's questions and cross-references peer.

**CTO**  (Round 2)

The path forward requires a tactical, two-pronged architectural approach: immediately wrapping existing systems with an Anti-Corruption Layer (ACL) to prevent catastrophic failure while rapidly deploying features. Concurrently, we must build a dedicated, isolated sandbox environment that serves as the controlled testing ground for CMO's segment experiments without touching core infrastructure.

**Agreements:**
- With the CEO: I agree that we must define a non-negotiable minimum viable architecture floor immediately to prevent catastrophic failure.
- With the CFO: I agree that technical implementation costs (specifically, refactoring and infrastructure) cannot be treated merely as sunk costs; they must be modeled as necessary pre-requisites for future ROI calculation.

**Conflicts:**
- CMO vs CTO: The CMO’s focus on *market* segmentation is fine, but it must be strictly constrained to the data available and testable within the Sandbox environment. We cannot use the sandbox as an excuse to bypass core architectural plumbing.
- CFO vs CTO: The CFO's view of 'Data Corpus' needs immediate technical clarification; I interpret this not as a repository, but as defined, governed API contracts and service boundaries.

**Required Changes:**
- The CEO must mandate that the CMO’s scope selection is based on *technical feasibility* within an isolated sandbox environment, not purely market potential.
- The CFO must fund the necessary foundational work (ACL/Gateway implementation) as a critical 'Platform Enabler' cost center, separate from feature development budgets.

**Key Findings:**
- The current systemic risk is not feature scope, but architectural coupling. The proposed ACL/Strangler Fig pattern allows rapid MVP deployment (CMO goal) while mitigating immediate failure risks (CTO mandate).
- Building the isolated sandbox first confirms that we can de-risk market experimentation without prematurely committing to a full microservice rewrite or creating vendor lock-in on shared services.
- The conflict between 'fast/focused' and 'slow/deep' is not mutually exclusive; it dictates an iterative, layered architectural rollout. We must prioritize the foundational plumbing (APIs, data contracts) over feature parity.

**Risks:**
- The ACL/Gateway introduces an immediate point of failure and latency overhead. Mitigation requires rigorous performance testing against projected peak load.
- Relying on the Sandbox creates a temptation to keep features siloed, leading to integration complexity debt when services must communicate later. Mitigation: Define common service contracts from Day 1.
- The technical debt incurred by quick ACL wrapping (the 'band-aid') might mask deeper data model inconsistencies, making future refactoring harder. Mitigation: Budget dedicated time post-MVP for deep data schema cleanup.
